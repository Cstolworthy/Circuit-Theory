Lesson 3: Logic Gates

Introduction

Logic gates are the fundamental building blocks of digital circuits. They implement boolean functions, taking binary inputs (0 or 1) and producing binary outputs according to specific rules. Understanding logic gates is essential for designing and analyzing digital circuits, from simple LED controls to complex microprocessors in robotic systems.

In this lesson, we'll explore the basic types of logic gates, their truth tables, symbols, and how they can be combined to create more complex digital functions.

Basic Logic Gates

1. NOT Gate (Inverter)

The NOT gate, also called an inverter, has a single input and produces the opposite (complement) of that input.

\text{Symbol: } A \rightarrow \overrightarrow{\text{|>o}} \rightarrow Y \text{ (where } Y = \text{NOT } A \text{ or } \overline{A}\text{)} \begin{array}{|c|c|} \hline A & Y \\ \hline \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{1} \\ \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{0} \\ \hline \end{array}

The NOT gate reverses the logic state - if the input is TRUE (1), the output is FALSE (0), and vice versa.

2. AND Gate

The AND gate produces a high output (1) only when all of its inputs are high.

\text{Symbol: } A \rightarrow \boxed{\&} \rightarrow Y \text{ (where } Y = A \text{ AND } B\text{)}\\ \phantom{\text{Symbol: }} B \rightarrow \begin{array}{|c|c|c|} \hline A & B & Y \\ \hline \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{0} \\ \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{0} \\ \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{0} \\ \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{1} \\ \hline \end{array}

The AND gate implements logical multiplication: the output is 1 only if both input A AND input B are 1.

3. OR Gate

The OR gate produces a high output (1) when at least one of its inputs is high.

\text{Symbol: } A \rightarrow \boxed{\geq 1} \rightarrow Y \text{ (where } Y = A \text{ OR } B\text{)}\\ \phantom{\text{Symbol: }} B \rightarrow \begin{array}{|c|c|c|} \hline A & B & Y \\ \hline \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{0} \\ \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{1} \\ \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{1} \\ \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{1} \\ \hline \end{array}

The OR gate implements logical addition: the output is 1 if either input A OR input B (or both) are 1.

4. NAND Gate

The NAND gate is an AND gate followed by a NOT gate. Its output is the complement of an AND gate.

\text{Symbol: } A \rightarrow \boxed{\&\circ} \rightarrow Y \text{ (where } Y = \text{NOT}(A \text{ AND } B)\text{)}\\ \phantom{\text{Symbol: }} B \rightarrow \begin{array}{|c|c|c|} \hline A & B & Y \\ \hline \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{1} \\ \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{1} \\ \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{1} \\ \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{0} \\ \hline \end{array}

The NAND gate is particularly important because it is "functionally complete" - any boolean function can be implemented using only NAND gates.

5. NOR Gate

The NOR gate is an OR gate followed by a NOT gate. Its output is the complement of an OR gate.

\text{Symbol: } A \rightarrow \boxed{\geq 1 \circ} \rightarrow Y \text{ (where } Y = \text{NOT}(A \text{ OR } B)\text{)}\\ \phantom{\text{Symbol: }} B \rightarrow \begin{array}{|c|c|c|} \hline A & B & Y \\ \hline \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{1} \\ \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{0} \\ \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{0} \\ \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{0} \\ \hline \end{array}

Like the NAND gate, the NOR gate is also functionally complete - any boolean function can be implemented using only NOR gates.

6. XOR Gate (Exclusive OR)

The XOR (Exclusive OR) gate produces a high output when an odd number of its inputs are high.

\text{Symbol: } A \rightarrow \boxed{=1} \rightarrow Y \text{ (where } Y = A \text{ XOR } B\text{)}\\ \phantom{\text{Symbol: }} B \rightarrow \begin{array}{|c|c|c|} \hline A & B & Y \\ \hline \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{0} \\ \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{1} \\ \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{1} \\ \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{0} \\ \hline \end{array}

The XOR gate outputs 1 when the inputs are different, and 0 when they are the same. It's commonly used in arithmetic circuits.

7. XNOR Gate (Exclusive NOR)

The XNOR gate is an XOR gate followed by a NOT gate. Its output is the complement of an XOR gate.

\text{Symbol: } A \rightarrow \boxed{=1\circ} \rightarrow Y \text{ (where } Y = \text{NOT}(A \text{ XOR } B)\text{)}\\ \phantom{\text{Symbol: }} B \rightarrow \begin{array}{|c|c|c|} \hline A & B & Y \\ \hline \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{1} \\ \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{0} \\ \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{0} \\ \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{1} \\ \hline \end{array}

The XNOR gate outputs 1 when the inputs are the same, and 0 when they are different. It's essentially an "equality detector".

Note on Symbols: The ASCII representations above are simplified. In actual circuit diagrams, standardized symbols are used which vary slightly between American (ANSI/IEEE) and European (IEC) standards.

Logic Gates in Electronic Circuits

In physical electronic circuits, logic gates are implemented using transistors. For example:

  • A basic NOT gate can be constructed using a single transistor
  • An AND gate typically requires two transistors
  • More complex gates require more transistors

In modern integrated circuits (ICs), logic gates are available in standardized packages, such as:

  • 74xx series (TTL - Transistor-Transistor Logic)
  • 40xx series (CMOS - Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor)

For example, a 7408 IC contains four 2-input AND gates, while a 7432 contains four 2-input OR gates.

Boolean Algebra and Logic Gate Equivalents

Boolean algebra provides a mathematical foundation for working with logic gates. Some key identities include:

  • Double negation: \(\text{NOT}(\text{NOT } A) = A\)
  • Commutative laws: \(A \text{ AND } B = B \text{ AND } A\); \(A \text{ OR } B = B \text{ OR } A\)
  • Associative laws: \((A \text{ AND } B) \text{ AND } C = A \text{ AND } (B \text{ AND } C)\); \((A \text{ OR } B) \text{ OR } C = A \text{ OR } (B \text{ OR } C)\)
  • Distributive laws: \(A \text{ AND } (B \text{ OR } C) = (A \text{ AND } B) \text{ OR } (A \text{ AND } C)\); \(A \text{ OR } (B \text{ AND } C) = (A \text{ OR } B) \text{ AND } (A \text{ OR } C)\)
  • De Morgan's laws: \(\text{NOT}(A \text{ AND } B) = \text{NOT } A \text{ OR NOT } B\); \(\text{NOT}(A \text{ OR } B) = \text{NOT } A \text{ AND NOT } B\)

These laws allow us to simplify complex logic expressions and design more efficient circuits.

Combining Logic Gates

Logic gates can be combined to create more complex circuits with specific functionalities:

1. Half Adder

A half adder adds two bits and produces a sum and a carry bit:

\begin{array}{l} \text{Sum} = A \text{ XOR } B \\ \text{Carry} = A \text{ AND } B \end{array} \begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|} \hline A & B & \text{Sum} & \text{Carry} \\ \hline \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{0} \\ \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{0} \\ \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{0} \\ \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{1} & \textcolor{red}{0} & \textcolor{red}{1} \\ \hline \end{array}

2. Full Adder

A full adder adds three bits (two input bits and a carry-in) and produces a sum and a carry-out:

\begin{array}{l} \text{Sum} = A \text{ XOR } B \text{ XOR } C_{in} \\ \text{Carry-out} = (A \text{ AND } B) \text{ OR } (C_{in} \text{ AND } (A \text{ XOR } B)) \end{array}

Full adders are used to build multi-bit adders for arithmetic operations.

3. Flip-Flops

Combinations of logic gates can create memory elements known as flip-flops, which can store one bit of information. These are fundamental to sequential circuits, which have memory, unlike combinational circuits made solely from basic logic gates.

Logic Gates in Microcontrollers

In microcontroller programming for robotics, we often use bitwise operations to implement logic functions:

// C/C++ code examples // NOT operation result = ~A; // AND operation result = A & B; // OR operation result = A | B; // XOR operation result = A ^ B; // Example: Check if a specific bit is set if (PINB & (1 << 3)) { // Bit 3 is high } // Example: Set a specific bit PORTD |= (1 << 5); // Set bit 5 high // Example: Clear a specific bit PORTD &= ~(1 << 5); // Set bit 5 low

These operations allow direct manipulation of individual bits in registers, which is essential for controlling hardware in robotics applications.

Practical Application: Simple LED Circuit

Let's design a simple circuit that turns on an LED when two push buttons are pressed simultaneously (implementing an AND function):

\begin{array}{l} \text{Components:} \\ \text{- Two push buttons (Button A and Button B)} \\ \text{- One LED with current-limiting resistor} \\ \text{- One 2-input AND gate (e.g., 7408 IC)} \\ \text{- Power supply (e.g., 5V)} \end{array}

When both Button A AND Button B are pressed, the inputs to the AND gate are both HIGH, producing a HIGH output that turns on the LED. This simple circuit demonstrates the practical application of logic gates in electronic systems.

Extending the Circuit

We can extend this concept to create more complex behaviors:

  • Adding an OR gate to turn on the LED when either button is pressed
  • Using a NOT gate to create an inverted logic (LED is on when button is not pressed)
  • Combining multiple gates to create circuits that respond to specific patterns of button presses

Summary

In this lesson, we've covered:

  • The basic types of logic gates (NOT, AND, OR, NAND, NOR, XOR, XNOR) and their truth tables
  • How logic gates are implemented in physical electronic circuits
  • Boolean algebra principles for working with logic gates
  • How to combine logic gates to create more complex functions
  • Application of logic operations in microcontroller programming
  • Practical examples of logic gates in simple electronic circuits

Logic gates are the building blocks of all digital systems, from simple control circuits to complex microprocessors used in advanced robotics. Understanding how they work and how to combine them is essential for designing and programming digital circuits.
